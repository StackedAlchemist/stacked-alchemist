I came to programming the same way I came to building PCs — through curiosity, and a stubborn need to understand how things actually work. Not just that they work, but why. What's happening inside the box. What decisions someone made to make this thing do that thing.

I built my first PC in 2005. I was hooked immediately — not just on the hardware, but on the philosophy of it. Every component matters. Every choice has a consequence. Put the wrong parts together and nothing works. Put the right parts together and you have something that hums with a kind of quiet power.

Code turned out to be exactly the same — except instead of a screwdriver, all you need is a keyboard and enough patience to read an error message twice.

I started with HTML and CSS. Basic stuff — structure and style. I remember the first time I changed a color on a webpage and the browser instantly reflected it back at me. That moment of feedback, that immediate proof that I had made a thing do something — I was hooked in a way I hadn't expected.

What surprised me most was how therapeutic it turned out to be. I have a background in security — years of staying alert, watching for problems before they happen, keeping a calm head when things go sideways. Programming scratches a similar itch. There's always a problem to solve. There's always something that isn't quite right yet. And the moment you find it — the moment you see the bug, trace it to the source, fix it and watch the thing finally work — it's one of the most satisfying feelings I've found.

I'm still early in this journey. HTML, CSS, JavaScript, a bit of Python. I know enough to build things that work, and I know exactly how much I still don't know — which is the right place to be.

The best developers I've read about all say the same thing: the learning never stops. You just get faster at it.

This blog is part of that. A place to write about what I'm building, what I'm struggling with, what clicked today that didn't click yesterday. If you're on a similar road — welcome. Pull up a chair.

The compiler's always listening.
