The Winglord Saga — Book One

The inn had no name worth remembering, but it had everything worth having.

It perched on the southern edge of Stormreach Isle like something that had been blown there by a forgotten storm and simply decided to stay -- a rambling, beloved wreck of a building with beams that groaned in the night wind and floors worn smooth by a century of boots. The lanterns burned gold-warm in their iron sconces, and the smoke from the hearth had been soaking into the timber for so long that the walls themselves smelled of pine resin and spiced sky-ale. The innkeeper, a broad and patient man named Brannic, had been pouring drinks in this room since before most of his current customers were born, and he had long since developed the professional wisdom of not asking questions about the things he saw.

Tonight, that wisdom was being tested in ways he had not anticipated.

Eight people sat in his common room, and if you did not know what they were, you might have taken them for exactly what they appeared to be: travelers, road-worn and loud, gathered around the long central table with the particular ease of people who had been through enough together that silence between them was never uncomfortable and laughter was never far. They wore plain clothes. Their hands were empty. They looked -- every one of them -- like people who had left whatever weight they carried outside with the night air, and come inside to simply be.

Brannic knew better, of course. He had known the moment they walked in. You did not live on Stormreach Isle your whole life without learning to recognize the Winglords when they chose to be recognized, and there were eight of them in his common room, which was seven more than he had ever expected to see in one place, in one lifetime, and they were drinking his ale and making the rafters shake with the force of their laughter and he was trying very hard to act as though this were a normal evening.

Tulek Stormreach sat at the head of the long table, not because anyone had assigned him the seat but because that was simply where he ended up, the way water found the lowest point without being told. He was a broad-shouldered man with ash-colored hair and storm-gray eyes and the kind of face that had spent enough time in difficult weather to have developed opinions about it. He had the look of someone who laughed easily and meant it every time, and right now he was leaning forward on both elbows with his mug tipping at a dangerous angle in one hand, holding the table's attention with the focused energy of a man who had been waiting to tell this particular story for some time.

"And then," Tulek said, spreading his free hand wide to convey the appropriate scale of disaster, "the wyvern looks me dead in the eye. Does not look away. Does not back down. And sneezes. Right into my face, from a distance of approximately two feet, and I want you to understand that I am the strongest Winglord ever born and I could not do a single thing about it because I was laughing too hard to move."

He paused to let that settle.

"I smelled like burnt feathers for a week. A full week. Launa made me sleep on the exterior platform."

Hiel Emberlash gave up. He had been trying, visibly and unsuccessfully, to maintain some version of composure since the wyvern entered the story, and now he simply stopped trying and laughed the way he did everything -- without restraint, without apology, leaning so far back in his chair that it creaked a warning he ignored entirely. Hiel was built like a forge fire given a body and a personality to match: loud, warm, and entirely too much in enclosed spaces, with copper-bright hair that caught the lamplight and eyes that burned with the particular mischief of someone who had never once in his life thought a situation was unsalvageable.

"You," Hiel said, pointing at Tulek with the hand that wasn't pressed to his ribs, "are the apex of everything our kind represents. The pinnacle. The strongest Winglord ever born, as you just said yourself. And a baby wyvern sneezed on you."

"A juvenile," Veyra Nightcarve said, without looking up. She was seated midway down the table with the particular stillness she carried everywhere, like a blade left on a table -- present, precise, and not something you touched without intention. Her dark hair was pulled back with the efficiency of someone who considered it a distraction. She had been etching something into the tabletop with the tip of a knife that she had absolutely been asked to leave outside, and she had done what she always did with requests that did not align with her preferences, which was to ignore them completely. "Based on the wingspan you described earlier. A full-grown wyvern's sneeze would have done considerably more damage."

"Thank you," Tulek said, "for that contribution."

"I am always helpful," Veyra said, and turned her knife slightly to work on a new angle of whatever she was carving.

Launa Stormreach made the sound she had been making her entire life in response to her brother, which was somewhere between a sigh and something genuinely fond -- an exhaled patience that had never quite curdled into irritation no matter how many years it had been tested. She sat to Tulek's left with the composed elegance of someone who had grown up translating chaos into something navigable, her hair braided with delicate sky-feathers that caught the lamplight whenever she moved. She had the same storm-gray eyes as her brother and almost none of the same recklessness, and she loved him in the way that only people who have spent a lifetime watching someone be magnificent and catastrophic in equal measure can love another person.

"You attract trouble," she told him, nudging his shoulder with hers. "Disaster simply follows behind it to see what happens."

"I said he attracted disaster," Hiel said.

"You were being dramatic. I was being precise," Launa replied. "There is a difference."

Soryn Dawnspear spoke without looking up from the gauntlet he was polishing, because Soryn applied the same quality of attention to maintaining his gear as he applied to everything else, which was to say a great deal of it, continuously. He was the most composed person in the room in the way that a drawn bowstring was composed -- not relaxed, but controlled, every part of him in deliberate relationship with every other part. His pale blond hair was cropped with military precision and his amber eyes missed very little, even when they appeared to be aimed at a piece of armor.

"If you applied half the energy you spend on spectacle to the work," Soryn said to Tulek, "you would be genuinely unstoppable rather than theoretically so."

Tulek pointed at him. "I am unstoppable."

Soryn's polishing cloth moved in a slow, measured circle. "Mm."

"That is not a counterargument," Tulek said.

"It was not intended as one," Soryn replied.

Yunlosh Stoneback's laugh came from the far end of the table, low and rolling, the kind of sound that belonged to someone built the way he was built -- wide through the shoulders, steady in the way of things that have decided they are not going to be moved. He was the quietest of them by nature, slow to speak and slower to anger, with warm dark eyes that held the particular tiredness of someone who had been carrying something unspoken for a very long time. He laughed, and the sound was genuine, and then he went quiet again and lifted his cup and watched the others with the patient attention of a man who preferred to listen.

Maelor Frostgale had been quiet through most of it. He sat at the table's edge with his pale crystalline eyes aimed at the fire and some portion of his remarkable mind clearly elsewhere, turning over something private and unreachable in the way he often did when the conversation moved into territory he had already analyzed and filed. He was the scholar of them, the archivist, the one who understood things the others only felt, and right now he was doing something with the information in his head that he had not shared with anyone, and would not, and the firelight caught the angles of his face and made him look like something carved from winter.

Brannic moved behind his bar with the careful movements of a man trying not to disturb anything. His cloth had stopped pretending to wipe the same section of counter. He was simply holding it, watching, not quite able to look away.

But he noticed what the others at the table had not yet allowed themselves to notice, or perhaps could not notice from where they sat, which was the figure in the far corner.

Arlyn sat alone.

Her chair was angled toward the door, the way someone angles a chair when they expect to need to leave quickly. She had not touched her drink. She had not spoken since arriving. Her eyes were on the window and the cloud-sea beyond it and the faint, distant lightning that flickered beneath the island's edge and never quite reached the sky. She was watching the weather with the focused attention of someone listening for something inside it, and whatever she was listening for, her expression said she had not decided yet whether she hoped to hear it or not.

Tulek noticed. He always noticed things that were wrong the way he noticed weather -- not by looking for it but by feeling it, a change in pressure, a shift in how the air moved. He saw Arlyn across the room and felt it in the way he felt approaching storms, that early recognition that something was coming that he could not yet name.

He opened his mouth.

Launa's hand came down on his arm, gently, before the words arrived.

"Leave her," his sister said, softly, and her voice was so warm and certain that Tulek did not question it. He believed it was kindness. He believed it because he had never once had a reason not to believe Launa's kindness.

He settled back. He picked up his mug. He let Hiel pull him back into the story and he laughed when the story required it and the warmth of the room closed back around him like it had never parted.

He would think about that moment for a very long time, afterward. He would think about it in the way you think about the last ordinary thing -- the last cup of ale, the last laugh, the last time someone you trusted put a hand on your arm and said something gentle, and you believed them, because why would you not.

Outside, the wind shifted.

Tulek felt it even through the walls, through the warm amber noise of the inn, through the ale and the lamplight and his sister's voice. It moved across his awareness the way a wrong note moves through music -- not loud enough to stop everything, just wrong enough that some part of him that had learned to pay attention to wrongness came awake and stood very still and listened.

He looked at Launa.

She was already looking at him. Her brow had drawn together in the way it did when she was reading the currents, reaching into the invisible pressures that moved beneath the surface of things. The sky-feathers in her hair had gone completely still.

"Any word from the scout?" Hiel asked, and the question came out stripped of all its usual performance, just a careful question from a man who had felt the air change and did not want to be the one who said so first. "The one tracking the dragon."

The word moved through the room like cold through an open door.

Dragon.

Tulek set his mug down. The sound of it against the table was very quiet. "None. He should have returned hours ago."

"Dragons do not approach the Isles without purpose," Maelor said, from wherever his mind had been. He had not moved, but his pale eyes had sharpened, and the fire he was looking at seemed colder for it. "Or without direction."

Yunlosh said nothing. He set his cup down as well.

Launa's eyes closed. Her breath came out slow and deliberate, the way she breathed when she was letting herself feel something she had been holding at arm's length. When she spoke, her voice was very quiet and very steady.

"Brother," she said. "I feel a storm coming. A terrible one."

The door came apart.

Not opened. Not pushed or blown or thrown wide. It came apart -- struck from outside with something that was not a fist, slammed inward hard enough to tear one hinge entirely free of the frame and send the nearest lantern swinging in a wild arc that threw every shadow in the room lurching. The figure in the doorway would not have been recognizable as the scout he was if Tulek had not spent months learning to recognize him in any condition.

He had seen the man in combat. He had seen him in weather that would have broken ordinary soldiers. He had never seen him like this.

The scout moved the way a thing moves when the only reason it is still moving is that it has not yet received permission to stop. Each step was a separate decision. His right arm hung at an angle that meant something had been done to the shoulder that could not be undone easily. His face was burned along one side in a pattern Tulek recognized from aether-discharge, not fire, something more precise and much worse. He crossed the threshold and the momentum that had carried him this far finally ran out, and he began to go down.

Tulek was out of his chair.

He crossed the room in the time it took the scout's knees to buckle, and he was there when the man's weight gave out, catching him, lowering him to the floor with the focused care of someone who understood that some injuries became fatal the moment you moved them wrong.

"We have you," Tulek said. "Stay still. Tell me what you need to tell me."

The scout's mouth worked. The words came in pieces, pushed out through breathing that was wrong in its rhythm, too shallow, too fast.

"My lords," the scout managed. "The drag--"

The roof came down.

It did not creak. It did not give warning. The second scout hit it from above with the force of something that had been thrown rather than dropped -- not falling but propelled, accelerated, aimed -- and the timber frame split under the impact, and the man came through it in an eruption of broken beam and thatch and cascading debris that tore the center of the ceiling open in a ragged wound. He hit the floor three feet from the table. The sound of it was the kind of sound that cleared a room of everything else entirely.

He did not move after.

His wings never came.

The debris followed him down in a slow secondary rain -- splinters of old timber, clumps of thatch, the shattered remains of the lantern that had hung at the ceiling's center, smoke beginning to curl from the edges where the flame had caught the exposed wood before the wind killed it.

Brannic hit the floor behind his bar.

And the Winglords became what they were.

It happened the way breathing happened, the way a hand closes around something falling before the mind has finished deciding to catch it. Armor arrived on them -- not from anywhere in the room, but from the Aether itself, materializing across their bodies between one instant and the next the way certainty materializes. Tulek felt the familiar weight of his own settle across his shoulders, his chest, his arms, each piece arriving in sequence so fast the sequence was invisible. His sword arrived in his right hand with the weight of something that had always been there and simply been elsewhere until this moment.

Then the wings came.

Seven pairs opened in the confined space of the common room, and the room could not hold them and did not try -- Hiel's blazed red-gold and the temperature surged; Veyra's swallowed light and the corners of the room went dark; Soryn's burned white-brilliant; Yunlosh's spread wide and gray and immovable; Maelor's trailed cold; Launa's opened with a dancer's precision, pale and translucent and perfectly placed; Tulek's crackled at the primary feathers with the faint charge that had always lived in them, lightning finding its preferred pathways.

Arlyn's wings opened in the far corner, midnight-black, and then she looked at the ceiling, at the dead scout, at the living one on the floor, and something in her face did a thing Tulek could not interpret from across the room.

"Ascend," he said.

They went up through the hole in the roof and into the storm that was waiting for them.

It was not a natural storm. Tulek knew the difference the way he knew the difference between a person walking toward him and a person walking toward him with intent. The wind came from directions it had no meteorological reason to come from. The lightning moved in patterns that suggested choreography rather than atmosphere. The clouds churned in a ring around a space of impossible stillness, as though the storm had been built to frame something, to create an arena out of open sky.

They broke through the cloud wall and the arena received them.

The dragon waited at the far end of the stillness, and it was patient in the way that things were patient when they had already won and were simply waiting for the rest of the world to realize it. Its chest pulsed with compressed aether-fire that it had been drawing from the storm's own energy, building something inside it that had nothing to do with the natural fires of a natural creature.

The Winglords spread into formation without being told. They had run this together a hundred times. The Octagon was the oldest arrangement they had, the one that turned eight separate strengths into something the sum could not predict, geometric and ancient and exact. Tulek took the anchor position and felt them settle into place around him, felt the lines of shared aether snap between them as the formation locked, felt the familiar hum of it rise through his chest.

He had stood in this formation his entire adult life.

"Hold the line," he said. "Let it strike. Channel everything through."

The dragon fired.

The beam that came from it was not fire, not in any sense that fire meant something manageable. It was annihilation reduced to a color, red-white and total, and it crossed the stillness between them faster than anything that large had any right to move.

Tulek steadied the formation. Felt the aether surge from all of them into the sigil, felt the geometry of the Octagon begin to do what it was built to do.

Felt it twist.

The wrongness arrived in the center of his chest, the place where the formation's joined power always settled when he anchored it. Not a disruption. Not interference from outside. A redirection from within, subtle and precise, requiring not one set of hands but several working in concert, adjusting the geometry of eight people's combined strength by fractions that were invisible individually and catastrophic in aggregate.

Pointing it at him.

He said her name.

It came out wrong. Too quiet, too confused, not yet angry, still looking for the explanation that would make sense of what he was feeling, still searching for the misunderstanding that would resolve this into something other than what it was.

"Launa."

Her eyes met his across the formation.

She did not look away.

The beam struck the sigil and the sigil bent it, and it came for him with the patience of something that had been arranged very carefully over a very long time.

The agony was not the worst part.

The worst part was that he understood. Even as his armor shattered and his right side became a report he could not process and his wings spasmed once and then went somewhere he could not reach them, he understood. He understood the whole shape of it, the why and the how and the when it had been decided, and it was that understanding that broke something in him that the beam itself could not have touched.

He fell through the bottom of the stillness and the storm took him.

The wind tore at him and the lightning reached for him and he fell through it all with his wings gone and his strength going and the darkness at the edges of his vision pulling inward.

Somewhere above him, a voice. Launa's voice, calm and certain and carrying the way her voice always carried through weather: "Stop him. He is starting the spell."

He was.

He had not decided to. His hands were moving, his lips shaping sounds that had nothing to do with decision and everything to do with the part of him that was not ready, that had never been ready, that would never be ready for this to be the end of it. The words were old. Older than he was, older than the Winglords, older than everything that had agreed they should never be spoken. They opened something. They always opened something, that was why they were forbidden, because the door they opened did not have the courtesy to close again afterward.

His soul recognized what his mind was doing and screamed at him to stop.

He did not stop.

He felt himself come apart at the seams, the tether between soul and body fraying the way rope frayed when something had been pulling at it for too long from opposite ends. He had intended precision. He had intended to send himself somewhere specific, somewhere the Winglords would not think to search, somewhere he could wait in whatever form remained to him until he understood enough to act.

He had not intended to misfire.

But precision required a mind, and his mind was going, and what remained was only the raw insistence of something that refused to stop existing. His soul left his body with all the grace of a stone thrown in desperation -- not aimed, not controlled, simply away, as hard and as far as the last of him could manage.

The world went dark.

The world stayed dark.

He was not anywhere for a very long time.

High above the storm's guttering remains, perched on a narrow shard of cloud as though it had been placed there specifically for this purpose, a small figure in a patchwork coat sat with his legs hanging over the edge of the world and watched the darkness settle over the sky. He was not large. He was not visibly powerful. He had the kind of face that collected wrinkles the way old wood collected grain, each one suggesting years without quite adding up to an age, and his eyes were the particular brightness of someone who finds almost everything interesting and almost nothing surprising.

He tilted his head. He watched the place where Tulek had been.

"Well," he said, conversationally, to the empty air. "That did not go as planned."

He was quiet for a moment, letting the last of the lightning die. The storm was coming apart without the Winglords inside it to give it shape, the choreographed winds losing their arrangement and going back to being ordinary weather, the darkness thinning toward the ordinary darkness of a sky-island night.

"The spell misfired," he said to himself. "He was too far gone for it to land where he intended." He paused. "Which means the soul is in the Aether. Drifting. Looking for something strong enough to hold it." Another pause. "That will take time."

He appeared to run some internal calculation.

"A thousand years," he said. "Possibly more. Possibly considerably more, depending on what becomes available." He did not seem distressed by this. He seemed, if anything, interested. "And then it will need to find its footing inside a vessel that does not know it is a vessel. And then the vessel will need to find its footing in a world that is not going to make that easy." He smiled. "And then it gets interesting."

He snapped his fingers once, a small gesture without ceremony, and the last of the storm went out.

The sky over Stormreach Isle went quiet.

He sat a moment longer, looking at nothing in particular, in the way of someone whose thoughts were running significantly ahead of the present moment and finding it good.

"All of this," he said quietly, "will rest on their shoulders. And they will not know it. Not for a long time. That is the part I have always found most remarkable about them." His expression held something that was not quite fondness and not quite pity and was perhaps the thing that lives between the two when someone very old watches someone very young walk toward something very large without understanding its size.

He stood up.

He stepped off the cloud.

The sky held its breath.

Somewhere far below, scattered in the places where lives were quietly being made, a thousand years were just beginning their patience. And at the end of all that patience, in a village that did not exist yet, a boy was waiting to be born.

He did not know it yet.

The storm inside him did not know it yet either.

But the storm was coming.

It was always, already, coming.
