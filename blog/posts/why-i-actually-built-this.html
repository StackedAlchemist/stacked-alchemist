People look at the apps. They see the portfolio. They see the terminal animation and the dark aesthetic and the list of deployed applications and they think they understand what Stacked Alchemist is.

They don't. Not yet.

So let me tell you why I actually built this. Not the business reason. The real reason.

The Filing Cabinet

I've described my mind before as a filing cabinet. Everything locked away behind walls, behind gates, behind a whole facility designed to keep things contained. Things seep through the cracks sometimes. But mostly it holds.

I built that filing cabinet young. Real young.

I was five years old when my mom left my dad. What I know about why is pieced together from fragments of memory and things I was told over the years. What I remember is walking down a hallway as a little kid, watching things get destroyed. That's one of my clearest early memories. Not a birthday. Not Christmas. That hallway.

Most of my childhood after that is a misty fog. We moved constantly. Porterville, California with no car, walking everywhere. Washington State on a bus with nothing. Back to Arizona. I remember stubbing my toe so many times crossing the same street back and forth. I remember earthquakes. I remember apartments that weren't ours and people who were only in our lives for a season.

I grew up in complicated family circumstances that I won't spell out here — partly to protect people I love, partly because the details matter less than the feeling they left behind. The feeling of ground that was never quite solid. Of not being sure what was true. Of carrying questions that didn't have clean answers.

What Nobody Tells You About Growing Up Like That

Nobody tells you what it does to a kid to have no stable ground. No safe adult. No place that stays the same long enough to feel like home.

You learn to lock things away. You learn that feelings are a liability. You learn that the only person you can count on to keep moving is yourself. You build walls not because you're broken but because walls are how you survive.

I used to practice what I call pain transference. I know that's not easy to read. But I'm writing it because I think there are people reading this who understand exactly what I mean. When emotional pain has no shape, no location, no way to be seen or touched or healed — sometimes you find a way to make it visible. To give it a location outside of yourself. To watch it exist somewhere real, somewhere you can see it, and feel the emotional weight drain out with it. It wasn't about wanting to die. It was about needing the pain to have somewhere to go.

I have a tattoo on my left shoulder now. A shield. A skull. A crescent moon and a sun. A sword and a rose crossing diagonally. It covers the deepest scars.

The meaning I gave it: Protector of love, through time and death.

That's not just ink. That's a decision. A decision to put something meaningful over the evidence of the hardest years. To transform what was there into something that belongs to me on my own terms.

The Things That Shaped the Mission

When I was 16, I woke up one morning and my mom was just — gone. Arrested overnight. I was a kid sitting alone in an apartment trying to figure out what came next by myself. I found out my aunt had passed away that same period — got the call on my way to school one morning, sat with it for a moment, shed some tears, wiped them, put my backpack on and went anyway. Because what else do you do.

I ended up bouncing around. Stayed with my mom's coworker for a while — a very strict woman, bars on the windows, rules that didn't bend. I came home too late one night and was made to sleep outside as punishment. Woke up with red, hot bumps all over me from whatever bit me out there in the dark. A friend's mom gave me penicillin. Thankfully I wasn't allergic.

My mom eventually got out. It wasn't a rescue. It was just putting her back in the same situation that created the problem in the first place. Some people cause the fire and then hand you a glass of water and call it heroism.

I moved seven times in one year. I turned 18 and got a job at Walmart and tried to go to school and moved in with my uncle, where I paid more than half his rent. When he was done with me — for whatever reason — he handed me a shotgun and a six-shot revolver and that was that.

I didn't have anywhere to put any of it. No one to call. No place that was safe. No space to sit down and say — this is what's happening to me and I don't know what to do with it.

I just locked it away and kept moving. Because that's what you do when there's no other option.

And Then I Lost My Son

My wife and I lost our son at 37 weeks. No heartbeat. We went in and he was just — gone.

I had to be the rock. I held it together for her because that's what I do. That's what the filing cabinet is for. You lock it away and you function and you be what the people you love need you to be.

About a month later I finally broke.

I tell you that not for sympathy. I tell you that because it was in that breaking — in finally feeling the weight of everything I'd been carrying for decades — that I understood something clearly for the first time.

There is an enormous number of people walking around right now carrying things they have nowhere safe to put.

Veterans who came back from places most of us will never go and were told to just readjust. Kids who grew up in chaos and learned to lock it all away. Parents running on empty who hold everyone else together while quietly falling apart. People who were raised to deal with it and move on — and did — and are still paying the price for it decades later.

They don't need a clinical app. They don't need a wellness platform. They don't need to be told to practice gratitude or download a meditation.

They need somewhere safe. Somewhere private. Somewhere that doesn't judge them for how they say it or what they say or how long it takes to say it.

They need what I never had.

That's Why Vault Exists

Not because I saw a gap in the market. Not because the numbers made sense. Not because it was a good business idea.

Because I know what it feels like to be 16 years old with no ground under your feet and nothing safe to hold onto.

Because I know what it feels like to carry a filing cabinet full of locked-away things for so long that you forget what it would feel like to set it down.

Because 22 veterans die by suicide every day and every single one of them deserved somewhere safe to put it before it got to that point.

Vault is free for everyone. Free forever for veterans. Not because it's a good marketing strategy. Because it's the right thing to do. Because if this app had existed when I was a kid — when my mom was struggling — when those veterans were sitting alone with things they couldn't say out loud — maybe things would have been different.

I can't go back. But I can build something that means the next person doesn't have to carry it alone.

The Shield Still Holds

I'm not broken. I want to be clear about that.

I have a wife who I love. Kids I'm trying to show up for better every day. A mission that gets me out of bed with something to build toward. A filing cabinet that's still full — but that I'm slowly learning to open, one drawer at a time.

The tattoo on my shoulder covers the scars. But it also tells the truth about who I became on the other side of all of it.

Protector of love, through time and death.

That's what Stacked Alchemist is. That's what Vault is. That's who's building this.

Not a polished founder with a Stanford degree and a venture capital check.

Just a kid from Mesa, Arizona who grew up in the fog, built walls to survive it, and decided to build something that means the next person doesn't have to.

If any of this hit you — if you recognized something in your own story — Vault is being built for you.
